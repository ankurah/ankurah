# Topic 6: staleness horizons and divergence bounding

Scope: this file feeds the #271 rejection-horizon decision and is a prerequisite for D3 implementation. Phase 2 retains full per-entity event DAGs with a retrieval budget as the only depth backstop; #271 proposes sealed-prefix checkpoints plus a concurrency rejection horizon so that a months-stale branch arriving today becomes an explicit policy decision (reject as too old, or route to rebase) rather than either a deep re-layering merge (thrash) or a BudgetExceeded anomaly. The review examines how prior systems bound divergence and forget history, hunts for cases where a bounding policy created new anomalies (conflict resurrection, permanent partitions, resource-exhaustion attacks), and closes with a structured evaluation of the four candidate policies named in the README against two hard requirements: the accept-or-reject decision must be deterministic and convergent across nodes, and each candidate needs a DoS analysis covering forged parentage that forces deep merges or deep walks. The central tension throughout: any horizon that lets nodes disagree on accept-versus-reject is a permanent partition machine, and any horizon expressed in a unit an adversary can forge (wall clock, self-declared depth) is an attack surface.

## Sources

### Dynamo, section 4.4: vector clock truncation and its acknowledged anomaly

Amazon's Dynamo paper (SOSP 2007) bounds causal metadata directly: "When the number of (node, counter) pairs in the vector clock reaches a threshold (say 10), the oldest pair is removed from the clock" and Dynamo "stores a timestamp that indicates the last time the node updated the data item" to pick which pair is oldest. The authors then concede the cost: "this truncation scheme can lead to inefficiencies in reconciliation as the descendant relationships cannot be derived accurately," softened only by "this problem has not surfaced in production and therefore this issue has not been thoroughly investigated." This is the canonical example of a staleness bound that trades correctness for a size cap and then hopes the failure is rare. Note the truncation key is a wall-clock timestamp, which is precisely the adversarially manipulable signal #271's hard requirements are worried about.
Source: https://www.allthingsdistributed.com/2007/10/amazons_dynamo.html (and the paper PDF at https://www.cs.cornell.edu/courses/cs5414/2017fa/papers/dynamo.pdf)
Verdict: CHALLENGES the "attested wall-clock horizon" candidate and, more broadly, any bound whose correctness rests on "has not surfaced in production." Truncating causal history to hit a size target can silently lose the descendant relationship that ankurah's causal comparison depends on for StrictDescends. AGREES with #271's premise that budget-shaped bounds are the wrong shape.

### CouchDB _revs_limit: depth truncation, the lost-common-ancestor hazard, and deterministic winner selection

CouchDB caps revision-tree depth with _revs_limit. A maintainer clarifies the real semantics: "_revs_limit is the depth limit, it doesn't limit the total number of revisions"; the tree is flattened into leaf-to-root paths, each path trimmed to the depth limit, then remerged. The documented hazard is exactly the ankurah identity-anchor concern in another system: if truncation discards the common ancestor two replicas need, replication cannot establish their relationship. Separately, CouchDB resolves conflicts convergently without a wall clock: "By default, CouchDB picks one arbitrary revision as the 'winner', using a deterministic algorithm so that the same choice will be made on all peers." The failure mode operators report is conflict resurrection: a value that looked saved reappears or is superseded "when conflicts are resolved in a separate sweep process," and heavily conflicted documents accumulate "hundreds of thousands of revisions" because shallow conflict branches never reach the depth threshold to be pruned.
Source: https://docs.couchdb.org/en/stable/replication/conflicts.html and https://groups.google.com/g/couchdb-user-archive/c/OhubNrFdc5Q
Verdict: CHALLENGES the naive form of #271 sealed-prefix pruning: a depth-based seal that drops the common ancestor breaks the very comparison it was meant to accelerate, which is why #271 must carry the genesis attestation. AGREES with the hard requirement's determinism clause: CouchDB shows a hash-style deterministic winner picked identically on all peers is achievable and is the correct model, matching ankurah's content-hash tiebreak.

### Riak dotted version vectors: sibling explosion from retried and interleaved writes

Riak's move from vnode version vectors to dotted version vectors (DVV) is the record of a divergence-bounding bug. Before DVV, "retried or interleaved writes could cause the number of sibling values to grow without bound, even if clients resolved siblings before writing," because a coarse version vector "loses some essential causal information" and treats a client's own follow-up write as concurrent, generating "false concurrency." DVV attaches a discrete dot to each sibling so an incoming context that "descends the dot stored with that value" supersedes it precisely, and crucially the metadata is bounded: it scales "with the replication degree rather than growing unboundedly with write patterns." Underlying paper: Preguica, Baquero, Almeida, Fonte, Goncalves, "Efficient causality tracking in distributed storage systems with dotted version vectors" (2012).
Source: https://riak.com/posts/technical/vector-clocks-revisited-part-2-dotted-version-vectors/index.html and the code/paper at https://github.com/ricardobcl/Dotted-Version-Vectors
Verdict: EXTENDS the causal-comparison design and #266. The lesson is that losing per-event granularity to save space manufactures false concurrency, which in ankurah terms would turn a StrictDescends into a spurious DivergedSince and force needless merges. A seal (#271) that collapses many events into one snapshot must preserve enough dot-like provenance that post-seal comparisons do not read as false concurrency; this reinforces #271 carrying per-backend provenance and #266's applied-set index granularity.

### Matrix state resolution v2 (MSC1442): depth is untrustable, and the retreat to a pure function

Matrix's second state-resolution algorithm is a direct retreat from depth-based ordering, and its reasoning maps onto ankurah almost line for line. The proposal states plainly that "depth of an event cannot be reliably calculated without possessing the full DAG, and cannot be trusted when provided by other servers," because "servers are not required (or expected) to store the whole room DAG." The replacement ordering uses origin_server_ts plus mainline ordering, chosen partly because with a wall clock "end users can clearly see when a server is using incorrect values," an accountability property depth lacks. Most important for our determinism requirement: the algorithm is designed to be "effectively a pure function from sets of state to a single resolved set of state." Rejected events are handled uniformly by the algorithm rather than by ad hoc special cases.
Source: https://github.com/matrix-org/matrix-doc/blob/master/proposals/1442-state-resolution.md
Verdict: CHALLENGES the notion of a self-declared depth or generation number as a trustworthy horizon signal when it can arrive from an untrusted peer, and thereby CHALLENGES a naive "generation-distance horizon" if the generation number is ever taken from the wire rather than recomputed locally. AGREES strongly with #271's and #267's determinism requirement: the resolution/decision must be a pure function of graph facts, exactly ankurah's "resolution may depend only on graph facts and event contents, never wall clocks or arrival order."

### Matrix room v2 mainline ordering: deterministic total order from objective properties plus a hash tiebreak

The room v2 specification defines the concrete deterministic order that replaced depth: events are compared first by mainline proximity, then "x's origin_server_ts is less than y's," then "x's event_id is less than y's," where event_id is a SHA-256-derived identifier. This cascading tiebreak reaches a total order using only objective, verifiable event properties and terminates on a cryptographic hash, so all servers converge on identical orderings without trusting any single server's clock as authoritative for the tiebreak. This is a working proof that a convergent decision function over a DAG is buildable and that a content-hash final tiebreak is the standard way to guarantee it.
Source: https://spec.matrix.org/v1.1/rooms/v2/
Verdict: AGREES with ankurah's existing content-hash tiebreak in the LWW tournament and with the #271 determinism requirement. EXTENDS it with a concrete template: if a horizon decision ever needs a tiebreak, terminate it on the EventId hash, never on arrival order or a raw clock.

### Matrix depth abuse limits (Synapse commit): capping self-declared depth

Synapse hardened depth against abuse because it is attacker-controlled: the commit "Apply some limits to depth to counter abuse" caps a newly created event's depth to MAX_DEPTH = 2^63 - 1 and, on receipt, rejects any event whose depth is absent, non-integer, negative, or above the cap. This is defensive input validation on a number an adversary supplies, and it treats an implausible depth as a reason to reject the event outright rather than to trust it.
Source: https://github.com/matrix-org/synapse/commit/33f469ba19586bbafa0cf2c7d7c35463bdab87eb
Verdict: EXTENDS #274 (validated ingress) and the #271 DoS analysis. If ankurah ever admits any peer-supplied distance-like field, it must validate it defensively as Matrix does. Reinforces the position that a horizon signal is only safe if it is locally recomputed or cryptographically bound, not merely asserted.

### Matrix fake prev_events: forged parentage as a resource-exhaustion attack (the core DoS case)

This is the most on-point source for #271's forged-parentage DoS requirement. When events carry parents that do not exist (in Matrix, MSC2716 batch-send used artificial prev_events), those parents "are treated as unresolved backward extremities that the homeserver attempts to fetch during federation backfill" and "will never be found," so the backfill scheduler, which "prioritizes oldest backward extremities," keeps "picking the 5 unresolvable fake events in the room to backfill from," which "would stop us from backfilling any other events in the room." Scaled up, "thousands of batches per room" produce "thousands of unresolvable backward extremities." The mitigation that followed was exponential backoff on failed backfills (PR #13635): retries are spaced "2hr, 4hr, 8hr, 16hr, etc." with an upper bound, so an unfetchable parent costs bounded work instead of endless expensive federation calls into _get_state_ids_after_missing_prev_event.
Source: https://github.com/matrix-org/synapse/issues/11091 and https://github.com/matrix-org/synapse/pull/13635
Verdict: CHALLENGES "retrieval budget as the only backstop." Matrix shows that forged or dangling parentage does not merely spend a one-shot budget; without an explicit horizon it re-attracts the scheduler on every pass and starves legitimate work. The eventual fix is time-boxed backoff plus refusal to keep chasing, which is the operational shape of a rejection horizon. This is the single strongest argument that budget-only is insufficient and #271 is needed.

### Aleph, the DAG fork-bomb attack: local validation admits unbounded forged ancestry

Gagol, Lesniak, Straszak, Swietek, "Aleph: Efficient Atomic Broadcast in Asynchronous Networks with Byzantine Nodes" (2019) names the general attack behind the Matrix incident. In any DAG protocol where a unit is admitted after only "local validation" (checking that its parents are correct based on data in the unit and its parents), "a group of malicious nodes can create an exponentially large number of forking units, which all must be accepted by honest nodes," so that honest nodes "which end up downloading them are likely to crash or at least slow down significantly because of exhausting RAM or disc memory." Aleph's mitigation is non-local: units are disseminated by reliable broadcast (a validation mechanism that is not purely local), and "there can be only a single copy of a unit created by a particular node in a particular round," bounding fan-out at the source.
Source: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1908.05156 (also https://www.arxiv.org/pdf/1908.05156v1)
Verdict: CHALLENGES both budget-only and any purely local horizon check. Content-addressing gives ankurah collision-resistant identity, so an adversary cannot forge a specific EventId, but it does nothing to bound the number or depth of well-formed forged-ancestry events an attacker can mint. A horizon that is checked using only the incoming event and its claimed parents (a local check) is still vulnerable to a fork bomb. This argues the horizon must combine a structural clamp (a seal below which parents are refused, #271) with per-source rate limiting at ingress (#274), not a budget.

### Cassandra gc_grace_seconds and ScyllaDB repair-based tombstone GC: wall-clock horizons resurrect deleted data

Cassandra's tombstone lifecycle is a wall-clock horizon and a documented anomaly generator. Tombstones may be collected once older than gc_grace_seconds (default 864000 seconds, 10 days), the window existing "to provide time for the cluster to achieve consistency via repairs and prevent the resurrection of deleted data." If a replica misses a delete and repair does not complete before the deadline, the tombstone is purged elsewhere while the stale replica still holds the value, and on recovery "Cassandra treats the object on the recovered node as new data and propagates it," a zombie. ScyllaDB's response is to replace the wall-clock deadline with a structural condition: "we automatically remove tombstones, perform GC, only after repair is performed. This guarantees that all replica nodes have the tombstones," so "there is no more data resurrection if repair is not performed in time." The stated reason the time-based version fails is that repair is a low-priority task that "might miss the deadline" under load.
Source: https://www.scylladb.com/2022/06/30/preventing-data-resurrection-with-repair-based-tombstone-garbage-collection/ (background: https://cassandra.apache.org/doc/latest/cassandra/managing/operating/compaction/tombstones.html)
Verdict: CHALLENGES the "attested wall-clock horizon" candidate directly and AGREES with the "sealed-prefix structural horizon" candidate. This is the clearest cross-system evidence that tying a forget-or-reject decision to a clock deadline resurrects data when a node falls behind, and that the fix is to tie the decision to a proven-synced structural condition. It maps onto #271: seal or reject based on structural reachability (all relevant peers past the seal), not on age.

### MongoDB oplog window: the industrial precedent for reject-then-forced-resync

MongoDB's replica set defines "stale" structurally against a bounded log: "A replica set member becomes 'stale' when its replication process falls so far behind that the primary overwrites oplog entries the member has not yet replicated. The member cannot catch up and becomes 'stale.'" The recovery is not a merge but a reset: "you must completely resynchronize the member by removing its data and performing an initial sync." This is precisely the product-level treatment of a stale client that the README asks about: past a bounded history window, the system refuses incremental catch-up and forces a full resync, deterministically, because the log entries are objectively gone rather than judged too old by a clock.
Source: https://www.mongodb.com/docs/manual/tutorial/resync-replica-set-member/
Verdict: AGREES with #271's rejection horizon and the reject-or-rebase routing. It validates the shape "past the retained window, reject the incremental path and force a full re-fetch," and it makes the decision structural (the entry is gone) rather than temporal (the entry is old), which satisfies the determinism requirement.

### Bayou: tentative versus committed writes and the convergent decision anchor

Terry et al., "Managing Update Conflicts in Bayou" (SOSP 1995), is the origin of the tentative-versus-committed distinction and the discipline behind the determinism requirement. Servers apply writes tentatively on receipt; a designated primary assigns each a permanent Commit Sequence Number (CSN), and "the CSNs define a total order for committed updates" that "all nodes will eventually agree on," with "conflict detection and merge procedures deterministic so that servers resolve the same conflicts in the same manner." Committed writes are stable: no tentative write is ever ordered before a committed one.
Source: https://mwhittaker.github.io/papers/html/terry1995managing.html (and the SOSP 1995 paper)
Verdict: AGREES with the #271 determinism requirement and is relevant to #272 (the ValueEntry::Pending question). Bayou is the reference implementation of "a below-horizon or committed prefix is stable and identically ordered everywhere." The caution it carries for ankurah: Bayou achieves this with a single primary that stabilizes order. Ankurah has no primary, so a stable sealed prefix must be reached by content-addressed agreement (Matrix-style pure function), not by a coordinator, or the seal boundary itself becomes a point where nodes can disagree.

### git commit-graph generation numbers (v1 and corrected v2): the reachability guarantee and a mixed-version corruption bug

Git's generation numbers are the reference design for the "generation-distance horizon" candidate. A commit's generation is "one more than the maximum generation number of its parents," giving strict monotonicity and the reachability guarantee "if gen(A) < gen(B) then A cannot reach B," which lets a reachability walk "stop visiting commits with generation number lower than the generation number of Y" and prune early. But generation numbers do not eliminate full walks: for merge-base and topological sort the algorithm "still requires walking the entire graph." Two further cautions bear on cross-node determinism. First, v1 topological levels can be badly pessimistic (merge-base v4.8 v4.9 walked 635,579 commits with topological levels versus 167,468 with committer dates), which is why v2 corrected committer dates exist. Second, mixing generation-number versions corrupted graphs in practice: Git v2.36.0's fix "surfaced corruption when running with a mixture of commit-graphs generated by Git v2.35.0 or earlier and Git v2.36.0 and later," because "using the newest available generation number from each layer would lead to violated expectations."
Source: https://devblogs.microsoft.com/devops/supercharging-the-git-commit-graph-iii-generations/ and https://git-scm.com/docs/commit-graph (bug context: https://gitlab.com/gitlab-org/gitaly/-/merge_requests/4677)
Verdict: EXTENDS #266 and CHALLENGES a naive "generation-distance horizon." The reachability property is real and useful for a soft horizon, but two things must hold for it to be a convergent decision signal in ankurah: the generation number must be recomputed locally from the DAG (never trusted from the wire, per the Matrix depth lesson), and every node must use the same generation definition, because git's own history shows that two definitions of the number in the same population produce divergent, corrupt reachability answers. A generation-distance horizon is only convergent if the generation function is fixed and identical across all nodes and versions.

### Automerge and Yjs: CRDT history and tombstones are load-bearing and cannot simply be dropped

Two mature CRDT libraries independently report that you cannot cheaply forget history. Automerge's compaction "combines incremental changes into a snapshot without deleting the full history"; it only deletes incremental files a process "had previously loaded," and full history is required because "automerge is a CRDT" that reconstructs state by merging all changes. Maintainers note history truncation "has not been prioritized" partly because measured savings from dropping history "are not all that great." Yjs, when GC is enabled, replaces a deleted item's content with a lightweight GC struct that "only stores the length," but it must retain the deleted id-range as a tombstone "to maintain struct ordering" and "correct synchronization," because the state vector tracks (client, clock) pairs and "discarding IDs would prevent peers from correctly identifying which operations they're missing." Disabling GC (doc.gc = false) is required to restore old content, and undo managers that disable GC make document size "grow unbounded."
Source: https://automerge.org/docs/reference/under-the-hood/storage/ , https://github.com/automerge/automerge/issues/799 , https://github.com/yjs/yjs/blob/main/INTERNALS.md
Verdict: CHALLENGES the storage-savings motivation for #271 and EXTENDS its design constraints. Both systems keep a structural residue (a length-only GC struct, a retained id-range, the historical _revs list in CouchDB) precisely so that sync and ordering survive forgetting. This is exactly ankurah's genesis-attestation requirement generalized: a seal may drop event bodies but must retain enough structural identity (the seal clock and a genesis binding) for comparison and disjointness to keep working. It also tempers expectations: if Automerge finds the space win small, the primary justification for #271 is bounding traversal and turning stale arrivals into decisions, not saving bytes.

## Findings

1. [DoS, #271, budget-only] Forged or dangling parentage is not a one-shot cost. The Matrix fake-prev_events incident shows a scheduler that re-selects unresolvable parents every pass, starving real work, and the Aleph fork-bomb result generalizes it: local validation admits exponentially many well-formed forged-ancestry units. A per-comparison retrieval budget bounds one walk but not the adversary's ability to keep presenting fresh deep or wide forged ancestry across many walks. Budget-only fails the DoS requirement in the many-request limit.

2. [DoS, content-addressing, #274] Content-addressing solves identity forgery (an attacker cannot forge a chosen EventId) but not ancestry-volume forgery (an attacker can mint unboundedly many valid events with deep or wide claimed parentage). The forged-parentage DoS is therefore not neutralized by our hash ids; it needs a structural clamp plus ingress rate limiting, matching Aleph's non-local mitigation.

3. [determinism, #271, #267] Every convergent system reviewed reaches its stable decision as a pure function of objective, verifiable facts terminating on a deterministic tiebreak: Matrix (mainline order then origin_server_ts then event_id hash, "a pure function from sets of state"), CouchDB (deterministic winner "made on all peers"), Bayou (primary CSN total order with deterministic merge). Any ankurah horizon decision must be the same: a pure function of graph facts and event contents, never of arrival order or a raw wall clock. This is already ankurah's stated merge rule; the horizon must inherit it.

4. [wall clock, attested-wall-clock candidate] Wall-clock horizons resurrect data or lose causal links when a node falls behind the window. Cassandra gc_grace_seconds produces zombies when repair misses the deadline; Dynamo's timestamp-keyed truncation can drop the pair needed to derive descent. Two independent systems converge on the same verdict: a clock deadline is the wrong trigger for a forget-or-reject decision because lateness is not the same as safety, and clocks are adversarially settable.

5. [structural horizon, sealed-prefix candidate] The strongest positive evidence is for a structural horizon. ScyllaDB explicitly replaced the clock deadline with "GC only after repair is performed," and MongoDB rejects-then-forces-resync based on the objective fact that oplog entries are gone. Both make the decision on structural reachability, which is deterministic across nodes by construction because it does not consult a clock.

6. [pruning correctness, #271 genesis attestation] Depth-based pruning that drops a common ancestor breaks comparison: CouchDB warns replication breaks if the common ancestor is lost, and every CRDT library retains a structural residue (Yjs id-range tombstones, CouchDB historical _revs list, Automerge full change history) so sync survives forgetting. Ankurah's genesis attestation in #271 is the correct and necessary analog; a seal must retain the seal clock and a genesis binding or Disjoint detection and StrictDescends grounding break.

7. [generation horizon, #266, generation-distance candidate] Generation numbers give a real early-cutoff reachability guarantee (gen(A) < gen(B) implies A cannot reach B) usable for a soft horizon, but git's own history shows two failure modes to avoid: v1 topological levels can be wildly pessimistic (a 3.8x walk blowup versus corrected dates), and two coexisting generation definitions corrupted reachability in a mixed population. A generation-distance horizon is convergent only if the generation function is fixed, identical across all nodes and versions, and recomputed locally rather than trusted from the wire.

8. [false concurrency, #266, #271] Collapsing per-event granularity to save space manufactures false concurrency: Riak's pre-DVV sibling explosion turned a client's own follow-up into an apparent concurrent write. A seal that folds many events into one snapshot must preserve enough dot-like provenance that post-seal comparisons do not misread descent as divergence, or #271 will trade storage for spurious merges.

9. [conflict resurrection, #271, #272] Truncation and deferred conflict sweeps cause user-visible resurrection: CouchDB users see a saved value vanish and reappear "when conflicts are resolved in a separate sweep process." Any ankurah rejection-horizon path that quarantines or defers a below-seal branch must define whether and when it can resurface, or it reproduces this anomaly. This connects to #272's tentative-versus-committed (ValueEntry::Pending) question.

10. [motivation calibration, #271] The storage-savings case for sealing is weaker than the traversal-bounding case. Automerge, which has every incentive to compress, reports the space win from dropping history is "not all that great" and deprioritized it. The load-bearing justification for #271 is therefore bounding traversal depth and converting stale arrivals from anomalies (BudgetExceeded) or thrash (deep re-layering) into explicit, convergent decisions, not byte savings.

## Candidate design deltas

- Adopt a structural horizon (#271 sealed-prefix) as the authoritative accept-or-reject mechanism, not a wall-clock horizon. Cassandra-to-ScyllaDB and MongoDB both demonstrate structural conditions are deterministic across nodes and do not resurrect data, whereas clock deadlines do both wrong. This is the review's primary recommendation for #271.

- Keep the retrieval budget, but demote it explicitly to an error-shaped safety valve, never a policy horizon. Matrix fake-prev_events and Aleph fork-bomb show a per-walk budget cannot bound a repeated forged-parentage adversary; the budget catches a single pathological walk, the horizon makes the policy decision.

- Pair the structural seal with per-source ingress rate limiting (#274). Content-addressing does not bound ancestry volume; Aleph's fix was non-local (one unit per creator per round). Ankurah needs an analogous bound at validated ingress so an attacker cannot mint unbounded forged-ancestry events below or across the seal.

- If a generation-distance soft horizon is used (#266, #271 point 4), fix the generation function immutably and recompute it locally from the DAG; never trust a wire-supplied generation or depth. Git's mixed-version corruption and Matrix's untrustable depth jointly show a distance number is only a convergent signal under a single, locally recomputed definition.

- Make the genesis attestation and seal-clock retention a hard invariant of any seal, validated on the seal path. CouchDB lost-common-ancestor breakage and the universal CRDT tombstone residue show a seal that drops structural identity breaks comparison and disjointness.

- Terminate any horizon tiebreak on the EventId hash, matching the existing LWW tournament and Matrix mainline ordering. If two nodes must decide identically at the seal boundary, the decision must be a pure function ending on the content hash, not on arrival order or a clock.

- Define resurrection semantics for the reject-or-rebase path up front (interacts with #272). CouchDB's separate-sweep resurrection is the anomaly to avoid; specify whether a rejected below-seal branch is dropped, quarantined, or rebased, and whether it can ever resurface, before D3 implementation.

## Policy evaluation

The four candidates are evaluated against the two hard requirements from #271: (R1) the accept-or-reject decision must be deterministic and convergent across nodes, and (R2) each candidate needs a DoS analysis covering forged parentage that forces deep merges or deep walks.

### Candidate A: retrieval budget as the only backstop (current)

R1 determinism and convergence: FAILS as a horizon. The budget is a local resource counter, not a decision function. Two nodes with different data locality, cache state, or the 4x retry landing differently will hit BudgetExceeded on different comparisons, so one node can complete an integration that another aborts. Per the README, a policy where one node rejects what another accepts is a permanent partition machine. The budget also produces BudgetExceeded, which #271 correctly calls an anomaly report rather than a decision, so it does not even attempt a convergent accept-or-reject.

R2 DoS: FAILS in the repeated-request limit. It bounds a single walk to a fixed number of fetches, which is genuinely useful against one pathological comparison. But the Matrix fake-prev_events incident shows forged or dangling parentage re-attracts the scheduler on every pass, and the Aleph fork-bomb shows an adversary can mint exponentially many valid forged-ancestry units. Each individual walk stays under budget while the aggregate lets the attacker spend our resources unboundedly across many walks and many entities.

Verdict: inadequate as the sole policy. Retain it as an error-shaped safety valve underneath a real horizon, not as the horizon.

### Candidate B: generation-distance horizon (reject when gen(head) minus gen(meet) exceeds a threshold)

R1 determinism and convergence: CONDITIONALLY PASSES, and only under strict discipline. Generation numbers are a deterministic function of the DAG (git's monotonicity and the gen(A) < gen(B) reachability property), so if every node computes gen identically and locally, gen(head) minus gen(meet) is the same everywhere and the threshold decision is convergent. The conditions are load-bearing: git's mixed-version corruption shows two coexisting generation definitions produce divergent reachability, and Matrix shows a wire-supplied depth is untrustable. If the generation number were ever trusted from a peer, or two node versions used different definitions, R1 fails.

R2 DoS: PARTIALLY MITIGATES, does not fully solve. A generation-distance threshold lets a node reject a branch whose meet is too far below its head without walking the whole gap, which caps deep-walk cost for the honest-history case and is a real improvement over budget-only. But an attacker can forge a branch with a small apparent generation distance yet large real breadth (many concurrent forged events at similar generation), forcing a wide merge inside the accepted window; generation distance measures depth, not breadth. It also does not by itself bound the number of distinct forged-ancestry branches presented. It needs ingress rate limiting to be DoS-complete.

Verdict: a sound soft horizon and the right early-warning signal (this is #271 point 4), but not a complete backstop on its own. Depends on #266 landing and on an immutable, locally recomputed generation function.

### Candidate C: attested wall-clock horizon (reject when the branch is older than a wall-clock window)

R1 determinism and convergence: FAILS unless the attestation is unforgeable and clocks agree, which is not a safe assumption. Even attested timestamps disagree across nodes at the window boundary, so a branch near the edge is accepted by a node with a slightly later clock and rejected by one with an earlier clock, reintroducing the permanent-partition risk. Matrix rejected depth partly because a peer-supplied ordering signal "cannot be trusted when provided by other servers"; a wall clock is the same class of untrusted, and additionally settable by the writer.

R2 DoS: FAILS on the forgery axis and creates a new anomaly. If the horizon trigger is a timestamp the writer controls, an adversary sets it inside the window to force acceptance and a deep merge, or outside to force rejection of a legitimate concurrent branch. Worse, Cassandra gc_grace_seconds and Dynamo timestamp-keyed truncation both demonstrate the deeper failure: a clock-deadline forget-or-reject decision resurrects deleted data or loses the causal link needed to derive descent when a node simply falls behind. ScyllaDB abandoned exactly this design in favor of a structural condition.

Verdict: reject. The cross-system evidence against wall-clock forget-or-reject decisions is the strongest negative result in this review. Wall clocks may inform advisory UX (Matrix keeps origin_server_ts for user-visible accountability) but must not gate a convergent accept-or-reject.

### Candidate D: sealed-prefix structural horizon (reject when an incoming event's parents fall below the seal clock C)

R1 determinism and convergence: PASSES by construction, provided the seal clock and genesis attestation are agreed and retained. The decision is "do this event's parents lie at or below C." Membership of an event id in the sealed prefix is an objective, content-addressed graph fact, not a clock reading, so any two nodes holding the same seal decide identically. This is the MongoDB pattern (the entry is objectively gone, so reject and force resync) and the ScyllaDB pattern (act on a proven structural condition), both of which are convergent because they consult structure, not time. The one genuine risk is disagreement about where C is, if nodes seal at different points; this is why sealing must be reached by content-addressed agreement and the seal must carry the genesis binding (CouchDB lost-ancestor breakage is the warning).

R2 DoS: BEST of the four, and completes the picture when paired with ingress limits.
